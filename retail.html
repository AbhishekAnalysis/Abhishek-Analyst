Project Overview

What & Why

Retail Sales Analysis is a two-page business intelligence dashboard built in Power BI, backed by a SQL data cleaning pipeline, to analyse sales performance across 12,362 orders spanning 2022 to 2024.

The goal was to move beyond surface-level revenue numbers — building a data story that equips business stakeholders to make decisions around category strategy, channel investment, seasonal planning, and location-based performance.

Every visual on the dashboard answers a specific business question, with YoY growth rates and category comparisons surfaced directly on each page.

Business Objectives

  • Track yearly and monthly revenue trends with growth rate annotations
  • Compare online vs in-store revenue contribution across periods
  • Identify top and bottom performing product categories
  • Understand the relationship between quantity sold and revenue by category
  • Provide an early 2025 January indicator without distorting 3-year comparisons

Challenges

Problems & How I Fixed Them

01
Inconsistent Dates & Nulls

Problem 01

Inconsistent Dates & Null Records

The raw retail dataset contained order date fields stored as mixed text and date types, NULL values in revenue and quantity columns, and orphaned records with no category mapping — all of which caused incorrect YoY growth calculations and broken bar chart aggregations in Power BI.

  • Order dates stored as VARCHAR instead of DATE — broke time-series grouping
  • NULL values in total_revenue and quantity_sold fields skewing aggregations
  • Orphaned order rows with no matching category — excluded from category charts silently
  • Duplicate order IDs inflating total order count beyond true 12,362

Fix: Wrote SQL cleaning queries — CAST(order_date AS DATE) to enforce type consistency, COALESCE to replace NULLs with 0, LEFT JOIN audit to flag orphaned records, and ROW_NUMBER() deduplication — producing a clean staging table before import into Power BI.

02
Partial-Year 2025 Distortion

Problem 02

Partial 2025 Data Distorting Trends

January 2025 data existed in the source table (₹25,875 revenue) but including it in the main 2022–2024 trend visuals caused the yearly bar chart to show a massive artificial drop for 2025 — misleading any stakeholder reading year-on-year growth.

  • 2025 (Jan only) pulled into the yearly aggregation, making it appear as a steep decline
  • No flag to distinguish full-year vs partial-year records in the source data
  • Monthly chart also affected — December 2024 vs January 2025 comparison was misleading
  • Needed a way to show the 2025 data without corrupting the 3-year trend analysis

Fix: Added a is_full_year SQL flag using a CASE WHEN YEAR(order_date) < 2025 condition, then filtered main visuals to full years only. A separate KPI card surfaces 2025 January revenue with an explicit "early indicator" annotation — matching the dashboard's "About 2025" callout.

03
Category Revenue vs Qty Mismatch

Problem 03

Category Names Mismatched Across Tables

Product category labels were stored inconsistently across the orders table and the product reference table — "Electronic household essentials" appeared as "Electronic HH Ess." in one table and "Elec. Household" in another — causing JOIN failures that silently dropped entire categories from the revenue vs quantity scatter chart.

  • 3 category name variants for the same product group across two tables
  • INNER JOIN on category name dropped unmatched rows silently — no error raised
  • "Computers and electronic accessories" missing entirely from scatter chart
  • Bottom 3 category rankings were incorrect due to missing data

Fix: Used SQL TRIM(LOWER(category_name)) normalisation on both tables before joining, replaced the INNER JOIN with a LEFT JOIN plus a WHERE matched IS NOT NULL audit step — ensuring all 8 categories appeared correctly in both the top/bottom rankings and the revenue vs quantity scatter.

Key Insights

What the Data Said

📈

Revenue Grew 6.95% in 2023

After a flat 2022 baseline, revenue grew 6.95% in 2023 before dipping 3.89% in 2024 — signalling a potential market saturation or competitive pressure that warrants investigation.

🥩

Butchers is the Top Category

Butchers leads all categories in revenue, followed closely by Electronic Household Essentials and Beverages — indicating that perishable goods drive the most transaction volume despite lower average prices.

🖥️

Computers Underperform vs Quantity

The Revenue vs Quantity scatter reveals Computers and Electronic Accessories generates lower revenue relative to units sold — suggesting either aggressive discounting or lower-priced SKU mix dominating the category.

🌐

Online Narrowly Leads In-Store

Online revenue (51.01%, ₹785.87K) edges out in-store (48.99%, ₹754.63K) — the near-parity suggests both channels are equally critical, and neither should be deprioritised in budget planning.

📅

March & December are Peak Months

Monthly revenue annotations show positive growth in March (+2.49%) and December (+5.96%), while September (-6.26%) and November (-7.47%) are consistent weak spots — ideal windows for seasonal promotions.

🥛

Milk Products & Patisserie Lag Behind

Milk Products and Patisserie consistently rank in the bottom 3 categories by revenue. These categories may benefit from bundling strategies or targeted promotions to improve their contribution margin.

What I Learned

Key Takeaways

SQL Before Power BI

Cleaning and normalising data in SQL before importing into Power BI keeps DAX measures clean and prevents silent data loss from failed JOINs — the foundation determines the accuracy of every visual built on top of it.

Partial-Year Data Needs Explicit Handling

Including incomplete year data in trend charts misleads stakeholders. Isolating partial years with a flag and presenting them separately as "early indicators" is a cleaner, more honest approach to evolving datasets.

INNER JOIN Silently Drops Data

Using INNER JOIN without auditing for unmatched rows is a silent data quality risk. Always validating JOINs with a LEFT JOIN + NULL check step caught missing categories that would have gone unnoticed in the final dashboard.

Revenue Alone Doesn't Tell the Full Story

Plotting revenue against quantity sold in a scatter chart revealed categories that were high-volume but low-value — a dimension invisible in a simple ranked bar chart. Combining metrics surfaces strategy-level insights.
